🎩 Hats: the vocabulary

Black hat

Acts without authorisation, for personal/criminal/political gain.

  • Illegal
  • Adversary you defend against

Grey hat

Acts without explicit permission but with non-malicious intent.

  • Legally risky
  • Often well-intended

White hat

Operates under written authorisation; reports findings.

  • Pen testers
  • Bug-bounty hunters
  • Red teams
The line: the action is the same; the authorisation makes it legal or not.
Make this very explicit. Even on systems learners "own" — corporate laptops, work apps — they may not have authorisation to test.

🥇 Becoming a white hat

  • Get a written, signed scope — always
  • Practice on intentionally vulnerable apps: Juice Shop, DVWA, HackTheBox, TryHackMe, Metasploitable
  • Participate in bug bounty programs (HackerOne, Bugcrowd, Intigriti) within program scope
  • Build the foundations: networking, web protocols, OS internals, cryptography, scripting
  • Study certifications as roadmaps: CEH (intro), OSCP (offensive practitioner), GIAC GPEN, OSWE
This course's path: we'll use Juice Shop and Metasploitable. Both are free and intended to be attacked.
Plug bug-bounty programs as the legal way to "do real attacks for real targets." Emphasise scope obsession.

🧠 The attacker's mental model

Three questions an attacker is always asking

  • What does this system trust? — users, certificates, headers, networks, environment vars
  • What does this system assume? — input shape, ordering, encoding, identity
  • What is at the boundary? — auth, parsers, deserialisers, subprocess, SQL
Developers ask "how do I make this work?" Attackers ask "what does this system do besides what's intended?"
Drill this in: every assumption is a potential vulnerability. Walk them through "what if I send a 10-MB request body" or "what if my filename contains a slash?"

🔍 Reconnaissance — passive vs active

Passive recon

No packets to the target. Hard to detect.

  • WHOIS, DNS, certificate transparency
  • LinkedIn, GitHub, leaked credentials (HIBP)
  • Shodan, Censys
  • Job postings (tech stack giveaways)

Active recon

Packets do hit the target. Detectable.

  • Port scans (nmap)
  • Banner grabbing
  • Directory brute-forcing
  • Spider / crawler
Authorisation note: active recon requires the same written permission as exploitation. Port scanning a system without permission is illegal in most jurisdictions.
Ask: how much could you learn about your own employer with five minutes of passive recon? Often unsettlingly a lot.

🎮 MITRE ATT&CK

A living catalogue of attacker techniques

14 tactics (the "why"), 200+ techniques (the "how"), thousands of observed real-world examples.

  • Tactics: Initial Access, Execution, Persistence, Privilege Escalation, Defense Evasion, Credential Access, Discovery, Lateral Movement, Collection, C2, Exfiltration, Impact…
  • Use it to drive detection coverage gap analysis
  • Threat-intelligence reports map to ATT&CK IDs (e.g. T1566 phishing)
Reference: attack.mitre.org — bookmark this. We'll come back to it in the threat-modelling module.
ATT&CK is the connective tissue between "thinking like a hacker" and operational defence. Don't go deep here — flag it.

🐛 Bug bounties & responsible disclosure

Bug-bounty platforms

  • HackerOne, Bugcrowd, Intigriti, YesWeHack
  • Each program has a written scope & rules
  • Rewards: from CVE credit to $$$$

Responsible disclosure

  • Report to the vendor; give them time to fix
  • Coordinate with CERT/CC if vendor is unresponsive
  • Public disclosure only after patch + reasonable window (90 days is typical)
Run a vulnerability disclosure policy on the apps your team owns — even without a bounty. Researchers will find issues; you decide whether they have a safe path to tell you.
If the org doesn't have a VDP, recommend security.txt as a 30-minute first step.

⚖️ The legal lines

  • US: Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA) — "exceeding authorised access" is a crime
  • UK: Computer Misuse Act 1990
  • EU member states: NIS2, plus national laws (e.g. StGB §202 in Germany)
  • Regulators care about data accessed, not just whether you "broke in"
  • When in doubt: stop, document, ask
Even good intentions don't immunise you from prosecution if you act outside written scope. This is not theoretical.
Mention the Aaron Swartz case, the Coalfire-Iowa pen test arrest. These get attention.
